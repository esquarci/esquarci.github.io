01 — Project overview

Designing a booking experience for a fictional airline.

The aim of this project was to design a website for Skyline, a fictional airline start-up. The brief specified that the online experience should be fast, easy, and intuitive — based on a deep understanding of the target users.

The end goal was to design and build a clickable prototype that can be tested with users, alongside a detailed set of wireframes that can be handed over to UI designers and then developers.

Everything in this case study has been completed by me. I am responsible for all research and UX design; the focus is on usability of the product. In a real-world scenario, this project would be passed over to UI designers and then developers.

02 — Problem

The flow works — until users hit the decision points.

While the overall booking flow is perceived as relatively simple, users encounter friction and confusion at key decision points — particularly around fare selection, pricing clarity, and add-ons.

The experience often requires extra cognitive effort to understand what is included, what is optional, and what the total cost actually is — which leads to hesitation, misinterpretation, or missed actions.

03 — User Interviews

Six usability sessions in total, coded end-to-end.

I ran and analysed two moderated usability tests with participants booking a real trip against their own criteria, using an existing flight-search product as the baseline. Each session was screen-recorded with think-aloud narration, then coded for friction points and behavioural patterns.

I was also provided with four additional usability test recordings — two users each running through two separate booking websites — which I analysed and coded using the same note-taking framework. The findings below triangulate across all six sessions.

05 — Key findings

Six patterns surfaced across the usability sessions.

Key observations triangulated across both usability tests and the affinity diagram. Each is paired with the impact on user behaviour.

F · 01

Fare selection is the biggest source of confusion.

Users struggle to understand fare names and what each option includes. Naming (e.g. “Essentials”) does not match user expectations, and important details like baggage allowance are not immediately clear.
Impact — users are unsure which option best fits their needs and may choose incorrectly or spend extra time comparing.

High severity
F · 02

Pricing lacks transparency across the journey.

Users are unclear whether prices are total or duplicated (e.g. outbound vs return). Add-ons like insurance or seats aren't always clearly reflected in the total, and there's no pricing guidance on things like the seat map legend.
Impact — users feel less confident in the final price and may double-check or hesitate before proceeding.

High severity
F · 03

Add-ons and extras create cognitive overload.

Users often skip extras (insurance, car rental) unless clearly relevant. Some add extras only in specific contexts — longer trips, family needs — and upsell banners are frequently ignored or misunderstood.
Impact — opportunities for upsell are missed, and users may overlook relevant options.

Medium severity
F · 04

Interface clarity issues lead to missed actions.

Subtle UI elements (faint checkboxes, small pop-ups) are overlooked. Users miss key actions like selecting seats or declining add-ons, and some labels are unclear (e.g. “As shown on document”).
Impact — users make errors, skip steps unintentionally, or feel uncertain about their selections.

High severity
F · 05

Strong expectations around checkout and automation.

Users expect a clear, final breakdown of flights and extras at checkout, autofill for details like payee info and country code, and transparency in discounts, memberships and payment options.
Impact — when these expectations aren't met, trust and perceived usability decrease.

Medium severity
F · 06

Flight selection works well.

Users find the flight selection layout clear and easy to compare. Side-by-side pricing and summary cards are effective, and the process feels straightforward up to this point.
Impact — this is a strong part of the experience and sets a good foundation to build from.

Positive signal

The booking journey is functionally effective but cognitively demanding.

Clarity — especially around fares, pricing and add-ons — is the main usability gap. Improving information hierarchy, naming and visual feedback would significantly reduce confusion and increase user confidence throughout the journey.

06 — Design goals

From findings to how-might-wes.

The findings resolved into four design goals that the app will be judged against:

HMW · 01

How might we make fare differences obvious at a glance?

Replace abstract fare names with clear inclusion summaries, surface baggage and flexibility on the card itself, and make comparison between fares feel direct.

HMW · 02

How might we make pricing feel trustworthy end-to-end?

Show one total that updates transparently as extras are added or removed. Clarify per-passenger vs trip total, and give price context for seat tiers and add-ons.

HMW · 03

How might we make add-ons relevant, not noisy?

Time and frame extras around user context — longer trips, family bookings, business travel — so the right prompts surface at the right moment, and the rest get out of the way.

HMW · 04

How might we make key actions impossible to miss?

Strengthen the visual hierarchy of critical controls (seat selection, opt-outs), rewrite ambiguous labels, and confirm choices with clear feedback before moving on.
